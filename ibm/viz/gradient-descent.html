Gradient Descent

Gradient descent minimises the cost function J(w) by repeatedly moving w in the direction opposite the gradient. This visualises J(w) = ½n Σ(w·x − y)² on the simple dataset y = 2x (true weight w* = 2).

What to Notice
  • Good LR (α = 0.3): The descent point moves smoothly down the parabola, converging to w* = 2. Each step is appropriately sized — not too big, not too small.
  • Too large (α = 1.5): Steps overshoot the minimum, bouncing from one side to the other. If α > 2 for this problem, it diverges. This is why LR is the most sensitive hyperparameter.
  • Too small (α = 0.005): The descent crawls. Hit Run and watch hundreds of iterations pass with tiny progress. In practice this means hours of GPU time wasted.
  • The gradient is proportional to distance from minimum: Near w*, the parabola is nearly flat — the gradient is small, so the step is automatically small. This is why gradient descent naturally "slows down" as it converges.
  • The fit line converges to y = 2x: Watch the right chart — as w → 2, the predicted line approaches the true generating process.
