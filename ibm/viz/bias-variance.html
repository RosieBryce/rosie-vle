Bias–Variance Tradeoff

A core ML concept and a common assessment topic. A model with high bias underfits — it's too simple to capture the true pattern. A model with high variance overfits — it memorises training noise instead of learning the underlying function. Adjusting model complexity moves you along this tradeoff.

What to Notice
  • Train error always decreases with degree: A higher-degree polynomial can always fit the training points better (degree = n−1 can pass through all n points exactly). But this doesn't mean it generalises.
  • Test error has a U-shape: It decreases as the model gets complex enough to capture the true pattern, then increases again as the model starts fitting noise. The minimum is the "sweet spot".
  • More noise → sweet spot shifts left: When data is noisy, simpler models generalise better because there's less signal to capture. Increase noise and watch the optimal degree drop.
  • The assessment link: Regularisation (L1/L2, Dropout, early stopping) adds a penalty for complexity, effectively moving the model left along this curve without changing architecture. Cross-validation finds the sweet spot empirically.
  • Overfitting symptoms: Low train loss, high val/test loss. The gap between train and test error is a direct measure of variance.
